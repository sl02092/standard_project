Good, honest result — and worth sitting with rather than glossing over: face refinement made things worse overall.
Aggregate: 0.2507 (box-centroid) → 0.3265 (face-refined) — that's roughly a 30% increase in error, not the improvement the hypothesis predicted. Per-frame: 2 of 6 got better, 4 got worse, and the "better" cases weren't dramatically better while some "worse" cases were quite bad (man: −0.194, woman in the white dress: −0.186).
Two things this disproves, and I want to be direct about the first one since it's my mistake:

I claimed Stage 3 would be a "safe no-op" for object frames — that's wrong, and the newspaper result proves it. newspaper went from 0.023 (excellent) to 0.145 — a real regression, on an object, not a person. That means RetinaFace found a face somewhere in that crop despite the target being a newspaper. In busy multi-person TV/film scenes (exactly VAT's domain), a GDINO box — even a reasonably accurate one — can still contain a bystander's face nearby, especially once padded 15%. "Any face in the crop" isn't actually a safe signal in socially dense scenes. I shouldn't have asserted that as safe without testing it, and now we have direct evidence it isn't.
The bigger question this raises: was the original hypothesis about why box-centroid underperforms even correct? I'd assumed GT sits near head/face level for person targets, based on the earlier production sample (GT y≈0.92, predictions clustering y≈0.61). But look at what happened on that exact clip here: face-refined moved the prediction to 0.465 — worse than box-centroid's 0.331. If GT is genuinely down near the bottom of frame, and a face detector naturally centers on the head (typically the top portion of a person's body box), refining toward the face moves the prediction further from GT, not closer. That's consistent with what we're seeing across most frames.

That points to a real possibility worth checking before doing anything else with this: VAT's "attended target" annotation may not consistently mark the target person's face. Chong et al.'s annotation convention for person-targets may place the coordinate wherever the annotator judged the subject's gaze to land on the target's visible body — which could be their hands, torso, or elsewhere, not reliably the face. If that's true, "refine toward a face" is solving for an assumption the data doesn't actually support, and box-centroid's rougher whole-body estimate is a better structural match to how GT is actually distributed.
Cheap way to check this before investing more time: pull up a handful of person-target frames' raw annotation crosshairs (literally your original Step 1 sanity-check tool) and just look — do the crosshairs cluster near visible faces, or land elsewhere on the body? That's a five-minute visual check that would tell you whether this mitigation has any legs at all, versus continuing to iterate blind.
My recommendation given where you're at today: treat this as a closed negative result for now, same spirit as Mitigation 1 — document it (the aggregate numbers, the newspaper contamination case, and the hypothesis about GT placement) as a limitation you investigated and why it didn't pan out, rather than sinking more of today into it. It's a legitimate, citable finding either way. Given the full hybrid run is still your actual Objective 2 evidence and you've got papers to read this afternoon, I'd park this here unless the quick visual GT check makes you want to pursue it further.
